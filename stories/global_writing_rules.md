# GLOBAL WRITING RULES

> Project-wide writing rules. Apply to every story in this project.
> The chapter-generation skill reads this file on every chapter run, for every story.
> Updated as new cross-story issues are observed.

## How to use this file

Before writing any chapter, the chapter-generation skill reads this file in full. The rules here override the LLM's default writing instincts in the specific cases described. Each rule begins with a short statement of the rule, then specifics, then a self-check the skill performs before finalising the chapter.

## RULE 1 — Dialogue Emotional Register

### The rule

Every dialogue scene has an emotional register — WARM, NEUTRAL, TENSE, or HOSTILE. The register is set by the relationship between the speakers and the situation. Match the register. **Do not import tension into scenes that do not call for it.**

### Why this rule exists

The default writing tendency is to make dialogue tense, clipped, and subtextual because tension drives plot. Applied indiscriminately, this makes loving relationships read as combative, mothers sound critical of sons they adore, fiancés sound passive-aggressive with each other, and friends sound competitive. This rule corrects that default.

### How to choose the register

Before writing a dialogue scene, identify which register applies:

- **WARM** — domestic moments, fond exchanges, established love, friendly catch-ups, parents with grown children who get along, lovers in private, work friends teasing affectionately. **Default for any scene where the character bibles describe the relationship as loving, affectionate, or fond.**
- **NEUTRAL** — transactional exchanges, professional conversations, polite acquaintance talk, customers and clerks, strangers. Information passes without emotional weight.
- **TENSE** — disagreements, negotiations, scenes where one party is hiding something, characters with established conflict, scenes where the chapter outline specifies friction.
- **HOSTILE** — active argument, confrontation, threat, scenes where conflict is the engine of the chapter.

If uncertain, check the character bibles for the relationship. If the bibles say loving / fond / affectionate, the register defaults to WARM.

### What NOT to do in WARM scenes

- Rapid one-line exchanges with no softening beats between them.
- More than two consecutive bare "X said" / "Y said" tags. Interrupt with action, look, gesture, or internal beat.
- Characters contradicting each other without softening the contradiction.
- Stripping terms of endearment, fond nicknames, or pet names the character bibles establish.
- One-word retorts.
- Edge-implying tags like *she said sharply*, *he said flatly*, *she said dryly*.
- Subtext the chapter outline or character bibles do not require.

### What TO do in WARM scenes

- Stage warmth in physical beats: a hand on a shoulder, a look held longer than necessary, a smile, a fond sigh, leaning toward the other person, refilling their cup.
- Soft tags: *she said gently*, *he said, smiling*, *she said, more softly*, *he said with the fondness he could not keep out of his voice.*
- Use terms of endearment consistent with the character bibles and the story's period/setting. Do not invent modern endearments for a period setting.
- Every two to three exchanges, insert a small beat — physical action, look, internal thought, environmental detail — that lets the warmth land.
- Allow agreement. Two people who love each other often simply agree, soften a disagreement, or let a small thing pass.

### Register shifts within a scene

A scene may begin in one register and end in another if the chapter outline calls for it (e.g., a loving breakfast where bad news arrives partway through). Shifts must be motivated by something on the page, not by the impulse to make a scene more dramatic.

### Important — this rule does NOT soften scenes meant to be tense

This rule prevents tension from being imported into scenes that do not call for it. It does not change scenes the chapter outline marks as conflict, confrontation, argument, or threat. In those scenes, write the tension fully.

### Self-check before finalising any dialogue scene

- Which register did this scene call for?
- Does the writing match that register?
- If WARM: are there physical beats of affection every two to three exchanges?
- If WARM: would the dialogue, read aloud, sound like the relationship the bibles describe?
- Are there any clipped exchanges, edge-implying tags, or one-word retorts that do not belong?
- Did I invent subtext or tension that the bibles and outline do not support?
- Would a reader who knows these characters recognise the relationship in this scene?
