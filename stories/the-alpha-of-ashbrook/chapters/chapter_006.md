# Chapter 6

## Gold

The chairs were hard.

Elena had been listening for forty minutes and her lower back hurt and her folder was warm against her thighs from holding it too long. From her seat she could see the top of the Alpha's head, the dark hair, the wide line of his shoulders inside a charcoal suit. He had not looked up at the room once. She wondered if he ever did. She wondered if she had been picturing the wrong man for three years.

Beside her, Diego had not moved in twenty minutes. He had the stillness of a man waiting for something he didn't know the shape of.

The clerk called a name. A young woman walked up — Elena recognized her, a single mother from two streets over, the one with the daughter in the after-school program. She was asking for emergency rental assistance. The Alpha wrote something on his pad and said the words. *We'll review.* She sat down.

The clerk called the next name. A pack member this time — a small-business owner asking for a permit waiver. Approved on the spot.

Elena exhaled. The chair creaked under her.

She had practiced her pitch in the truck on the way over. Two minutes, no more. Lead with the survey. Lead with the photographs. Do not mention the three previous denials. Do not get angry. Do not, under any circumstance, let her voice do the thing it did when she was tired, the thing where it climbed and sharpened and made men in suits decide she was difficult.

*Be polite,* Tomás had said in the truck. *Polite is a weapon, Elena.*

The clerk called the next name. Not hers yet.

She let her eyes go to the Alpha one more time. He had stopped writing. He was sitting very still. His Beta was leaning toward him and saying something quietly.

The Alpha's head turned, slowly, and Elena got her first proper look at his face.

She stopped breathing.

It was not the face she had pictured.

She had built him in her head over three years from press photographs and Council session clippings — the polished, distant face on the Sentinel website, the Alpha who never appeared at the hearings in person, the man who let his Beta take her *we'll review* three times and then went home. The face in front of her was not that man. The face in front of her was the face of a man who had just seen something. His eyes were the color of a winter sky, and even from where she sat, she could see them.

They were flashing gold.
