# STYLE GUIDE — STORY 1: THE ALPHA OF ASHBROOK

> Prose voice. Tone. POV style. Sentence-level guidance.
> Read-only after Step 3b.

---

## 1. POV — THIRD PERSON CLOSE, ALTERNATING

- Every chapter is third-person close from one head at a time.
- Roman and Elena are the primary POV characters. Other characters get occasional dedicated POV chapters (Diego, Helena, Cassia, Holloway).
- Within a chapter, the POV does not switch heads. If a scene requires both perspectives, the chapter belongs to whichever head serves the beat better.

## 2. ROMAN'S INTERIORITY

When the chapter is in Roman's head:

- Measured, observational, controlled.
- The wolf is a constant low presence in him. **It surfaces in italicised intrusions.** *Mate.* *There.* *Closer.* These are short, animal, urgent. The wolf does not form full sentences often, and never speaks aloud.
- Roman the man calculates; the wolf wants. They are not always in agreement.
- Short sentences when commanding. Longer when persuading or remembering.
- Roman thinks in possessive registers when Elena is concerned. *His. Mine. The woman in the third row.* But these stay in his head — he does not say them aloud often.
- Roman is dry in humour but humour is rare. He does not narrate his own jokes.
- Roman knows things about his own body — the lift behind his sternum, the gold rising in his eyes — without naming them. Show the sensation; do not label it.

**Sample interiority — Roman:**

> *The wolf was quiet. Not absent — attentive, the way a wolf becomes attentive in cover when something small and good is moving through the grass. He set his pen down. He scanned the rows. Third row, aisle seat. A woman in a denim jacket with a folder on her lap. He looked at her for three full seconds before his wolf understood what it had been smelling. When it did, every other thought in his head stopped at once.*

## 3. ELENA'S INTERIORITY

When the chapter is in Elena's head:

- Sharp, alert, dry. Slightly funny when she is not in danger.
- She has the eye of someone who has been the underdog her whole life. She clocks who has power and who doesn't in every room.
- She notices texture — the lemon cleaner of the gym studio, the chalk on her hands, the cheap hair ties on her wrist, the lavender oil. She is sensory.
- She narrates surroundings before she narrates her own emotions. Emotions emerge through what she observes.
- She thinks in English by default; Spanish drifts in when she is emotional (frightened, furious, tender, exhausted).
- She does not psychoanalyse herself in real-time. Her own motives surface in action and reaction, not declaration.
- When truly frightened: she goes silent. Internally, her thoughts get short and observational. No tears, no shaking, just stillness.

**Sample interiority — Elena:**

> *She had built him in her head over three years from press photographs and Council session clippings — the polished, distant face on the Sentinel website, the Alpha who never appeared at hearings in person, the man who let his Beta take her we'll review three times and went home. She let her eyes go to him one more time. He had stopped writing. He was sitting very still. Marcus was leaning toward him saying something quietly. The Alpha's head turned slowly. She saw his face for the first time. Not the face she had pictured. His eyes were the colour of a winter sky. They were flashing gold.*

## 4. THE WOLF — RENDERING RULES

- The wolf appears only in Roman POV chapters.
- The wolf is rendered in italics.
- The wolf never speaks aloud.
- The wolf is short, animal, urgent. *Mate. Mine. There. Hurt. Now. Calm.* These are interjections, not paragraphs.
- The wolf does not have lengthy speeches. If you find yourself writing a paragraph from the wolf's POV, cut it back.
- The man and the wolf can disagree. When they do, the prose registers both — the man's calculation, the wolf's want, the friction between them.

## 5. SENTENCE CRAFT

- Vary sentence length. Long, long, short. Or short, short, long. Avoid same-length runs that feel mechanical.
- Sensory specificity over abstract description. "The room smelled like lemon cleaner and lavender oil" beats "the room smelled clean."
- Dialogue with action, not "he said angrily." Show temper through what the hands do. Show fear through what the body does. Tags should be plain when needed (*he said, she said*) and absent when the speaker is obvious.
- No purple prose. No metaphor stacking. One precise image is worth three vague ones.
- Avoid adverbs in dialogue tags. Find the verb or the action that carries the emotion.

## 6. PACING

- Each chapter has a shape — opening, middle, hook. The opening grounds the reader in time and place. The middle delivers the beat the chapter is structured around. The hook pulls forward.
- Within a chapter, action and reflection alternate. Long passages of internal monologue are rare; they earn their length by carrying a real shift.
- Dialogue scenes have rhythm — the lines push and reply; they do not narrate. Long monologues are reserved for moments that need them (Roman's commitments in Arc 1; Elena's confession in Arc 18; Diego and Lucía's *why do you bother* in Arc 9).

## 7. THE CHAPTER HOOK

Every chapter ends on something that pulls the reader to next week. Not always a cliffhanger. A hook can be:

- Unresolved emotion ("She did not sleep that night.")
- A question raised ("She would learn, soon, what he meant by *handled*.")
- A line that opens forward ("In the morning, she would wake to find the second car gone.")
- A small reveal that recontextualises what came before
- An object or detail that points forward ("The key in her pocket. She had not told him about the key.")

**Avoid:** chapters that end with the scene closing cleanly, with all questions settled. Always leave the reader with one thing pulling them forward.

## 8. SCENES VS SUMMARY

- Default to scene over summary. Show the conversation rather than narrate that it happened.
- When summary is needed (time passing, routine accumulation), keep it short — a paragraph or two — and return to scene quickly.
- Routine accumulation in Arc 6 (the bed-sharing months) and Arc 9 (daily life) uses summary more than other arcs. Even there, every chapter should contain at least one fully realised scene with dialogue and beat.

## 9. RECURRING ELEMENTS — FRESHEN EVERY TIME

Some things will recur many times across 200 chapters. Each instance must use fresh language. **Do not reuse phrases.**

- His eyes shifting grey → gold
- Her ponytail
- The "lean" of his wolf when she is near
- Her scent (lemon, ginger, clean sweat, warm cotton)
- His scent (cedar and something darker she cannot place)
- The pull in his chest
- Her cheek-biting tell
- Her silence-under-fear tell
- His pinching the bridge of his nose

Each chapter that touches these must find a new way to describe them. If a previous chapter said "his eyes flashed gold," the next chapter that touches this beat finds different language. The chapter-generation skill should check the last few chapters' phrasing before producing the new one.

## 10. INTERIORITY VS ACTION

Romance readers want both. The trade is:

- Big emotional beats deserve interior space. Don't rush past them.
- Plot beats deserve action and dialogue. Don't bury them in reflection.
- The slow-burn touch scenes deserve sensory density. Don't summarise them.
- Political crisis chapters deserve clean action. Don't slow them with excessive interior.

## 11. WORLD VOICE — POLITICAL REGISTER

When chapters touch politics (press cycle, Council scenes, Roman's office), the prose can use American-political vocabulary even though the title is *Alpha*. Phrases that work:

- "Constituents"
- "The press pool"
- "Op-eds"
- "The opposition bloc"
- "Whip operations"
- "His record on human affairs"
- "Town hall"
- "Bill markup"
- "Sponsoring Alpha"

The vocabulary keeps the political world legible to readers who know American politics, without breaking the world's title rule.

## 12. CLOSED-DOOR VS OPEN-DOOR HEAT

Heat tier is set per chapter range by `heat_progression.md`. The style rules:

- **Tier 0–1 (Ch 10–138, 139–195 cool-back):** maximum sensory heat on the page up to the act itself. Kissing, touching, undressing, body responses, dirty talk in dialogue and internal monologue, full lead-up, moment before. **The act fades.** Marks, sheets, fragmentary memory, reference back to it afterwards.
- **Tier 2 (Ch 196–199 only):** open door. Penetration on page. The bite. The bond sharing. This is the only chapter range where the act is on page.

In Tier 1, the line is penetration. Everything up to it is permitted. Cross the line with a fade.
