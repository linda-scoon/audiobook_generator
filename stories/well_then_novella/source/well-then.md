# WELL THEN

A complete adult contemporary romance novella draft.


# Chapter 1: Forgotten Birthday

By six in the evening, Clara Vale had stopped pretending the candles were for atmosphere.

There were six of them, low and ivory, set along the dining table in the Chelsea house Adrian had bought because the light through the back windows pleased her. He had noticed that once. He had stood in the empty room while the estate agent talked about marble, security, square footage, and resale value, and he had turned to Clara instead.

"You like the light."

She had loved him for that. Not the house. Not the money. The noticing.

At seven, the kitchen sent the beef back into the warmer.

At eight, the flowers began to look too formal for a private dinner. White roses. Her favourite when he remembered. His assistant had ordered them, of course. Clara knew because the card had said, Happy birthday, my love. A.V., and Adrian had not called her my love in writing since the first year of their marriage.

At nine, she blew out the candles before they burned down into sad little stumps.

The housekeeper hovered in the doorway with the look people used when wealth made pity awkward.

"Mrs. Vale, shall I clear?"

Clara folded the linen napkin over her lap once, then twice. "Not yet."

"Of course."

She waited until the woman left before she opened her phone again.

No missed calls.

One message from Adrian's office number at 6:14 p.m.

Running late. Eat without me. Important call with Singapore.

No apology. No birthday. No promise to come as soon as he could.

Clara had not eaten. She had sat through the slow death of the meal because some part of her still believed he might arrive with his tie loosened, his hair touched from running a hand through it, his mouth tired and sorry. He used to come in like that. He used to find her in a room and soften before he touched her, as though every hard thing in him went quiet when she looked back.

Now, when Adrian came home, he often kissed the air near her temple while reading a message on his phone.

At 9:37, the front door opened.

Clara looked up before she could stop herself.

Adrian entered with his coat over one arm and his phone against his ear. He was tall, dark-haired, beautifully dressed, and so familiar to her body that pain moved through her before affection did. His suit looked expensive and untouched. His face looked focused. He nodded once to the night guard, then crossed the hall without noticing the dining room until he was almost past it.

Then he stopped.

His eyes moved over the table: candles, wine, untouched food, her blue dress.

A flicker. Not guilt, not properly. Recognition.

"I'll call you back," he said into the phone.

He ended the call and came to the doorway.

"Clara."

She hated how much she still liked the sound of her name in his mouth.

"You missed dinner," she said.

His gaze dropped to the table again. "I know. I'm sorry. Singapore ran long, and then Martin needed the final numbers before New York opened. I should have texted again."

"Yes."

He loosened his tie. "Did you eat?"

"No."

That bothered him. She saw it. But irritation followed too quickly behind it, the kind he got when a problem had no efficient solution.

"Clara, you didn't have to wait. I told you to eat without me."

She stared at him.

He heard it then. Not everything, but enough to make him look more carefully at the table. He saw the flowers. The candles. The blue dress. He searched his memory, and the answer arrived late.

His face changed.

"Your birthday."

She looked down at her folded napkin.

"Clara."

"It's all right."

"No, it isn't. I forgot." He came to the table, pulled out the chair beside her, and sat. "I'm sorry. I genuinely am."

He reached for her hand. She let him take it because refusing would make a scene, and she was too tired to perform pain for him. His thumb moved over her knuckles, warm and absent-minded, as though apology had a technique.

"We'll go away this weekend," he said. "Paris, or the lake house. Anywhere you want."

She almost laughed. "You have Zurich this weekend."

"I'll move it."

"You won't."

His jaw tightened. "I said I would."

"And tomorrow something will happen at the office, and then something else, and then you'll say I understand because I always have."

Adrian sat back a fraction.

He was not a cruel man. That was the worst of it. If he had been cruel, she could have hated him cleanly. But he was busy, ambitious, adored, obeyed, and used to a world arranging itself around him. Somewhere in the middle of building a company large enough to swallow his days, he had let his wife become another permanent thing. The house was permanent. The marriage was permanent. Clara was permanent.

He did not chase what he thought he owned.

"This is about more than dinner," he said.

"Yes."

"Then talk to me."

She looked at him across the table he had missed. "Not tonight."

He did not like that answer. She saw the control in him rise, not anger exactly, but the instinct to manage. To turn pain into a meeting. To identify the issue and contain it before it damaged something expensive.

"Clara, I'm here now."

That was when she smiled. It surprised both of them. Small, soft, and emptied out.

"Yes," she said. "Now you are."

He flinched as if she had raised her voice.

The kitchen staff reheated the dinner. Adrian ate some because he was hungry, and Clara pushed food around her plate because her stomach had closed hours ago. He asked about her day. She answered. He promised again to make it up to her. She nodded. He watched her more than usual for the rest of the meal, which might have comforted her if it had not taken this to make him look.

Later, in their bedroom, he came out of the shower with damp hair and bare shoulders, and her body betrayed her by wanting him.

That had always been easy between them. Even when marriage frayed around the edges, heat remained. He had never neglected her in bed when he came to it. There, at least, Adrian was attentive, intense, possessive in a way that once made her feel chosen. But lately even sex had become something he reached for at midnight, tired and hungry for comfort, taking her warmth after giving the rest of himself away all day.

He stood behind her at the mirror and placed his hands on her waist.

"Come to bed," he said.

Her eyes met his in the glass.

She wanted to. That was the humiliation. Her skin remembered him. Her loneliness opened its mouth for anything that looked like closeness.

"I'm tired."

His hands stilled. "Of course."

He kissed her shoulder. Gentle. Too late.

In bed, he fell asleep within minutes.

Clara lay awake beside him and listened to his breathing. One year ago, he had woken her at midnight with a diamond bracelet and a crooked smile, because he had been too impatient to wait until morning. Two years ago, he had flown back from Milan early because she had sounded sad on the phone. Three years ago, he had married her in a small church outside Bath and whispered, "Nothing comes before you."

She turned her head on the pillow and looked at him.

He was still beautiful. Still hers by law. Still absent in every way that mattered.

In the morning, she made his coffee out of habit.

He kissed her on the mouth this time, distracted but real, and left with the cup in his hand.

"I'll call you," he said.

He did not.




# Chapter 2: Close Enough to Hurt

Celine March arrived at Vale Meridian with a smile that made reception straighten.

She was all gloss and precision: black suit, red mouth, pale hair cut sharp at her jaw. She had come from a European luxury group Adrian was trying to pull into a joint venture, and she treated business like flirtation and flirtation like business. Nothing she said crossed a line by itself. Everything she did touched it.

Adrian noticed. Of course he did.

He was not blind, and he was not innocent enough to pretend a beautiful woman leaning over his desk was merely checking figures. Celine laughed too softly at things that were not jokes. She put her hand on his forearm when making a point. She stood close enough that he could smell her perfume.

He should have stepped back.

Instead, he let it happen because it made the long hours feel less sterile.

That was the truth he did not name.

He had no intention of sleeping with Celine. He loved his wife. If anyone had asked, he would have said it immediately, almost offended by the question. Clara was not in competition with anyone. Clara was his wife, his home, the woman whose hair he still gathered in his fist when he wanted her face tipped up to his.

But Celine admired him in a way Clara no longer did openly. Celine looked at him as though he was powerful, fascinating, inevitable. Clara looked at him lately as though she was counting the minutes until he disappointed her again.

So when Celine leaned against the edge of his desk on a Thursday evening and said, "You know, Adrian, men like you are wasted on conference rooms," he did not shut it down.

He smiled.

"Where should I be instead?"

Her eyes moved over his face. "Somewhere with a view. And no minutes being taken."

He knew exactly what she was doing.

He told himself knowing made it harmless.

"Dangerous proposal from a woman asking me to sign a distribution agreement," he said.

"I would never mix business with pleasure."

"No?"

"Not without excellent terms."

He laughed then. Low, brief, real.

That was what Clara saw.

She had come to the office because she was tired of waiting for him to remember her. In her hand was a small paper bag from the bakery near their first flat, the one that made the pistachio cake he used to buy when they were newly married and pretending they could not afford it. It had been meant as an olive branch, though she had not admitted that even to herself.

She stepped out of the private lift into the quiet executive floor.

Adrian's assistant was gone. Most of the staff had left. The lights were lower than usual, city glass glowing black beyond the windows.

The door to Adrian's office stood half open.

Clara heard his laugh before she saw him.

Then she saw Celine sitting on the edge of his desk, knees turned toward him, one heel dangling. Adrian stood close enough that Celine's hand rested on his tie while she adjusted it with slow, intimate fingers.

"There," Celine said. "Much better. You looked almost human for a moment."

Adrian did not move away.

Clara's grip tightened around the bakery bag.

It was not a kiss. It was not his hand on Celine's waist. It was not enough to satisfy a court, perhaps. But it was enough to satisfy every lonely dinner, every forgotten birthday, every time Clara had slept beside the cool shape of him after midnight and wondered what part of his life still wanted her in it.

Celine saw her first.

The other woman's smile changed, not vanishing, only sharpening. "Mrs. Vale."

Adrian turned.

For one second, something like panic crossed his face.

Clara wished she had not seen it. Panic looked too much like guilt.

"Clara," he said. "What are you doing here?"

She heard the question before he did.

Not I'm glad to see you.

Not Come in.

Not This isn't what it looks like.

What are you doing here?

Celine slid off the desk with graceful leisure. "I was just leaving."

"Stay," Clara said.

Both of them looked at her.

Her voice sounded calm, which felt absurd when something inside her was tearing in a slow, wet line.

"I'm only dropping this off." She placed the paper bag on the small table by the door. "I thought you might not have eaten."

Adrian moved toward her. "Clara, wait."

She stepped back.

The movement stopped him more effectively than a shout.

"Enjoy your evening," she said.

Then she turned and walked to the lift.

He followed her. "Clara."

She pressed the button.

"Don't do this here," he said quietly.

That finished something in her.

She looked at him then. Really looked. At the expensive shirt Celine had touched. At the tired, handsome face that cared very much about privacy and timing and not making a scene. At the man who had forgotten that scenes happened inside people too.

"No," Clara said. "I wouldn't want to embarrass you."

The lift opened.

He put a hand against the door before it could close. "I am not having an affair."

She looked up at him.

"Did I ask?"

His mouth tightened. "You came in at the wrong moment."

"Clearly."

"Celine is a business partner."

"Then perhaps she should stop dressing your tie."

He had no answer quickly enough.

The lift tried to close. His hand kept it open.

"Come home. We'll talk there."

"Will you be home?"

The silence was tiny. Damning.

"I have to finish this meeting," he said.

Clara nodded once.

"Of course you do."

"Clara."

"Move your hand, Adrian."

For a moment she thought he would refuse. His fingers curled against the steel. His eyes had gone hard, not at her, she thought, but at the situation refusing to obey him.

Then he stepped back.

The lift closed between them.

Clara did not cry until the car reached the ground floor. Even then it was silent. Tears slipped down her face while the security guard pretended not to see, while the driver opened the rear door, while London slid by in bright, indifferent streaks.

At home, she changed out of the cream silk blouse she had chosen because Adrian once said it made her look soft and dangerous at the same time. She washed her face. She sat on the end of the bed and waited.

Nine o'clock.

Ten.

Eleven.

A message arrived at 11:18.

We need to talk. I’ll be home late. Don’t go to sleep angry.

Don't go to sleep angry.

She stared at the words until they blurred.

There was no I love you. No I left the meeting. No You matter more than this.

Clara stood.

Her suitcase was in the dressing room, cream leather, a gift from him before a holiday he had taken calls through. She opened it on the bed and began to pack.

At first she was careful. Clothes folded. Shoes in bags. Toiletries sealed. Then her hands began to shake, and she threw things in without order: jeans, jumpers, underwear, the red dress he liked, then took it out again and left it on the chair.

She removed her wedding ring last.

Not because she wanted to leave it. Because she did not.

It took her ten minutes to make her fingers open.

She placed the ring on his bedside table, where he would have to see it if he came to bed. If he came to bed at all.

By midnight, the taxi was waiting outside.




# Chapter 3: Well Then

Noel Price found Clara in the hall with two suitcases, a coat over her arm, and the face of a woman who had already broken before anyone heard the sound.

Noel was not a butler, though visiting guests sometimes mistook him for one because he had keys to everything and a habit of appearing exactly when a practical thing needed doing. His official title was Senior Estate Manager for Vale Residential Holdings, which meant he kept the Vales' houses alive whether they were occupied or not. He hired gardeners, checked boilers, arranged cleaners, oversaw security upgrades, prepared country houses before weekends, and knew which roof in which county would start leaking if ignored through November.

He had come to the Chelsea house because the alarm system in the wine room was throwing false alerts again.

He had not expected to see Mrs. Vale leaving her marriage at twelve minutes past midnight.

They looked at each other across the polished hall.

Clara's eyes were swollen. Her chin was steady.

Noel glanced once at the suitcases, then at the ring missing from her left hand.

He did not ask questions. Good staff knew when not to become witnesses too loudly.

"May I help with those, Mrs. Vale?"

Her mouth trembled before she controlled it. "Thank you, Noel."

He took the larger suitcase. It was heavier than he expected. He carried it down the front steps to the waiting taxi while the driver rose too slowly from behind the wheel.

"I'll take it," Noel said, not sharply, but with enough tone to hurry him.

The driver opened the boot.

Rain had begun, fine and cold, silvering the pavement. Clara stood beneath the portico in a camel coat, hair loose around her shoulders, looking back once at the house.

It was a beautiful house. Noel had prepared it for them after the wedding, had arranged the first delivery of white roses for the master bedroom because Mr. Vale had remembered. He had seen Mrs. Vale arrive laughing, barefoot after too much champagne, while her husband carried her over the threshold in a gesture so old-fashioned Noel had pretended not to watch.

Now Mr. Vale was absent, and Noel was loading his wife's luggage into a taxi.

The smaller suitcase followed. Then her overnight bag.

"Is there anything else?" Noel asked.

Clara looked at him.

There were many possible answers. A life. A husband. The stupid pistachio cake still sitting on Adrian's office table, unless someone had thrown it away.

"No," she said. "That's everything."

Noel closed the boot.

She stepped down into the rain. For a second neither of them moved.

Then Noel gave her a small nod, not quite professional, not quite personal.

"Well then," he said.

Clara's face changed. A breath that might have become a sob became something else. Almost a laugh. Almost dignity.

"Well then," she said.

She got into the taxi.

Noel shut the door.

The car pulled away from the curb and disappeared into the wet black street.

Noel stood there until the taillights turned the corner.

Then he went back inside, walked to the master bedroom because the house now required checking, and saw the wedding ring on Mr. Vale's bedside table.

"Bloody hell," he said quietly.

He did not touch it.

Adrian came home at 1:43 a.m.

His driver had barely stopped before he got out, phone in hand, irritation sharpened by exhaustion. The meeting with Celine had gone badly after Clara left. Not because Celine had lost composure. She had tried, with appalling skill, to turn the incident into intimacy.

"Your wife seems very emotional," she had said.

Adrian had gone cold then.

"Do not speak about my wife."

Celine had lifted both hands in mock surrender. "Of course."

He had ended the meeting within twenty minutes, but not quickly enough. There had been calls to make, legal language to settle, a crisis in Singapore that was apparently not a crisis when exposed to direct questions. By the time he left the office, he was angry at everyone and ready to have the conversation Clara had refused him.

The house was too quiet when he entered.

Not asleep quiet. Empty quiet.

He knew before anyone told him.

Still, he called her name.

"Clara?"

No answer.

He went upstairs first, then into the bedroom. Her drawers were open. The wardrobe held gaps where her clothes had been. Her scent remained, warm and floral, cruelly present because she was not.

Then he saw the ring.

For several seconds Adrian did not move.

He looked at the small circle of platinum on the bedside table as if it were a thing placed there by mistake. Clara did not take off her ring. She twisted it when nervous, pressed the diamond against her lower lip when reading, tapped it against glasses when she was thinking. She did not take it off.

He picked it up.

It was still warm only because the room was warm. His mind rejected that.

Downstairs, Noel stood in the hall with the wine room report in one hand and the expression of a man preparing to be disliked.

"Where is my wife?" Adrian asked.

Noel did not flinch. "Mrs. Vale left shortly after midnight."

"Left where?"

"She did not say, sir."

"You let her leave?"

Noel's brows lifted slightly. "She is Mrs. Vale. I was not under the impression she required my permission."

Adrian stared at him.

The answer was correct. He hated it.

"Was she alone?"

"She took a taxi."

"To where?"

"I do not know."

Adrian's hand closed around the ring. "You didn't ask?"

"No, sir."

"Why the hell not?"

Noel was silent for a moment.

Then he said, "Because she looked as though the last thing she needed was another man asking her to explain herself."

Adrian took one step toward him.

Noel did not move.

The house seemed to hold its breath.

Adrian stopped. He was not a man who lost control in front of employees. He was not a man who shouted in halls. But something ugly and frightened had opened under his ribs, and it wanted a target.

"Get out," he said.

Noel nodded once. "I'll send the security report in the morning."

"Now."

Noel left.

Adrian stood alone in the hall of the house he had bought because Clara liked the light.

He called her.

It went to voicemail.

He called again.

Voicemail.

He called her mother, who answered on the fifth ring with thick sleep in her voice and immediate alarm when she heard his.

"Adrian? What's happened?"

"Is Clara with you?"

"No. Why?"

"Did she call you?"

"No. Adrian, what have you done?"

He closed his eyes.

"Nothing. I need to find her. If she calls, tell me."

"If my daughter comes to me in the middle of the night because of you, I will not be reporting her location like a delivery update."

"Marianne."

"Do not Marianne me. Find your manners before you find my daughter."

She hung up.

Adrian stared at the phone.

Then he began calling Clara's friends.

None of them answered. Or they saw his name and chose not to.

By dawn, he had called her twenty-three times.

By seven, he had not slept.

By eight, he was sitting at the dining table where her birthday candles still stood, burned down and cold.

That was where the housekeeper found him.

"Mr. Vale? Would you like coffee?"

He looked toward the kitchen before he remembered.

Clara made his coffee in the mornings.

Not always. Not because staff could not. She did it when she was awake before him, leaving it in the blue cup he liked because it fit his hand properly. Strong, no sugar, a little milk when he had slept badly. She knew from his face. He had never told her the system. She had learned him.

"No," he said.

The housekeeper hesitated. "Sir?"

He looked at the empty chair opposite him.

"No coffee."

For the first time in years, Adrian Vale went to work without it.




# Chapter 4: The Empty House

Clara did not go to her mother's house because Adrian would look there first.

She went to Nina's.

Nina Hart lived in a narrow townhouse in Islington with a green door, an overfed cat, and no patience for rich men who confused provision with love. She opened the door at 12:39 a.m. wearing pyjamas, a silk eye mask pushed up into her hair, and the expression of a woman ready to commit crimes.

"Did he hit you?"

"No."

"Did he cheat?"

Clara tried to answer. Failed.

Nina stepped aside. "Come in."

By two in the morning, Clara had cried until her face hurt. By three, Nina had made tea strong enough to qualify as medicine. By four, Clara slept in the spare room beneath a quilt that smelled of lavender detergent and cat hair.

She woke at ten to twenty-seven missed calls, twelve messages from Adrian, four from her mother, and one from Noel Price.

Mrs. Vale, I hope you arrived safely. I will not disclose anything unless legally compelled. N.P.

Clara stared at that longer than any of Adrian's.

Nina came in with toast. "He doesn't know you're here?"

"No."

"Good. Let him sweat."

Clara sat up. Her head throbbed. "I don't want games."

"It isn't a game to let a man experience one consequence."

"He's my husband."

"Yes. That is why he should have behaved better than a man trying to keep his favourite chair."

Clara almost smiled.

Then her phone lit again.

Adrian.

She turned it face down.

Across London, Adrian stood in his office and watched the call fail.

Again.

The city moved beneath his windows. People crossed the plaza with coffees in hand. Cars slid along the river. Inside Vale Meridian, directors arrived with tablets and problems. His assistant, Gemma, entered with the morning brief and stopped when she saw his face.

"Cancel everything," Adrian said.

Gemma blinked. "Everything?"

"Until noon."

"You have Celine March at ten."

"Cancel it."

Gemma's expression shifted, just slightly. She knew. Staff always knew more than employers thought.

"Of course."

Adrian turned from the window. "And Gemma?"

"Yes?"

"Celine March is not to be admitted to this floor without a scheduled meeting and another person present. Ever."

Gemma's face revealed nothing. "Understood."

It was the first correct thing he had done since Clara walked into his office.

It did not help.

By noon, he had called Clara's mother again and been told to stop harassing her. He had sent messages that grew shorter as he realized length made him sound defensive.

I did not sleep with her.

I should have moved away when she touched me.

I am sorry.

Come home and shout at me here.

Please call me.

At two, he went home because the office had become unbearable. Not from memory of Clara in the lift. From memory of Celine at his desk. He saw the scene differently now, not through his own knowledge of what had not happened, but through Clara's eyes.

Celine's hand on his tie.

His smile.

His first question.

What are you doing here?

He sat in the back of the car and felt something colder than guilt move through him. Recognition, perhaps. Late and useless.

The Chelsea house was worse in daylight.

Clara was everywhere because she had never demanded space loudly. She had taken it gently, through habits. A paperback facedown on the small table by the library chair. Her gardening gloves on a shelf near the back doors because she liked cutting herbs herself even though the gardener found it inefficient. A blue scarf looped over a hook in the hall. A small dish of mints on his desk because he forgot to eat before meetings and she said mint helped when hunger made him sharp.

He had thought she did little because nothing in the house required her labour.

Now he saw she had been maintaining something less visible than a property.

A life.

He went into the kitchen. The chef looked at him with controlled terror.

"Did Mrs. Vale leave instructions for dinner?" Adrian asked.

The chef hesitated. "She usually discusses the week on Mondays."

"With you?"

"Yes, sir."

"Every week?"

"Most weeks. She asks about your schedule. Light meals if you have late calls. Proper breakfast if you're flying. No shellfish before board meetings because you don't trust new suppliers when you're distracted."

Adrian said nothing.

The chef swallowed. "Sir?"

"Thank you."

He left before the man could see too much.

Upstairs, he opened the drawer where Clara kept stationery. It smelled faintly of her perfume. Inside were cards she had bought and not yet sent: birthdays, congratulations, condolences. She remembered everyone. His nephews. His oldest investor's wife. Gemma's promotion. Noel's mother's surgery.

He found a list in her handwriting.

Adrian, Zurich flight, black coat repaired.

Adrian, call dentist, reschedule.

Adrian, hates the new protein drinks, cancel delivery.

Adrian, anniversary, no meetings after six? Ask Gemma quietly.

He read the last line three times.

Their anniversary had been seven weeks ago.

He remembered coming home at 10:15 to find her in a dark green dress and the table set in the smaller dining room. He remembered kissing her, apologizing, taking a call before dessert. He remembered returning to find the candles out and Clara saying she had a headache.

He had believed her because belief was convenient.

Adrian sat on the edge of their bed with her list in his hand.

His phone rang.

For one savage second, he thought it was her.

It was Celine.

He answered.

"Adrian," she said, warm and amused. "I hear our meeting was cancelled. Should I be worried?"

"Yes."

Silence.

"That's blunt."

"Our professional relationship will continue only with counsel present until the contract is signed or terminated. If you touch me again, speak about my wife again, or imply intimacy between us in any setting, I will end the deal and absorb the loss."

Celine was quiet long enough to prove she had understood.

Then she laughed softly. "All this because your wife had a tantrum?"

Adrian's hand closed around Clara's list.

"My wife left me because I humiliated her. Do not mistake that for drama."

"And you think turning puritan will bring her back?"

"I think I should have respected my marriage before I was in danger of losing it."

Another silence.

When Celine spoke again, the warmth was gone. "How noble. How late."

"Yes," he said. "It is."

He ended the call.

That night, he did not go to bed. He lay on the sofa in the library because the bedroom had become a room where absence had weight. At 3:12 a.m., he woke reaching for Clara and touched empty leather.

He had given her everything, he had thought.

Houses. Accounts. Security. Cars. A name that opened doors.

In the dark, with his hand curled around nothing, Adrian understood for the first time that everything could still leave a person starving.




# Chapter 5: Address on the Papers

The divorce papers arrived six days later.

Adrian was in the Chelsea library, unshaven, wearing yesterday's shirt, reading an email he had not absorbed, when the courier came. Legal envelope. Heavy cream paper. His name typed with professional indifference.

He knew before he opened it.

Still, the words hit like violence.

Petition for dissolution of marriage.

Clara Elizabeth Vale, petitioner.

Adrian Nicholas Vale, respondent.

He read nothing else at first. His eyes fixed on her name, then on the address listed for service.

Nina Hart's house.

His body moved before thought finished forming.

He took his coat. His keys. Clara's ring from his pocket, because he had carried it there every day since she left. His driver was off duty; Adrian drove himself, badly and too fast, through wet London streets until he reached Islington close to midnight.

Nina Hart opened the door wearing a dressing gown and the expression of a woman who had expected him eventually.

"No," she said.

Adrian stood on the step, rain in his hair. "I need to see my wife."

"Your wife needs sleep."

"Nina."

"Mr. Vale."

The correction was deliberate. It landed.

He looked past her into the narrow hall. Warm light. Shoes by the stairs. A small cat glaring from a radiator. Ordinary life, closing ranks around Clara.

"Tell her I'm here."

"She knows."

His breath changed.

"Then let me speak to her."

Nina folded her arms. "You are not coming into my house to drag a woman into a midnight argument because the papers scared you."

"I have never dragged Clara anywhere."

"No. You just left her standing alone until she moved herself."

He absorbed that because he deserved it.

"Please," he said.

That word seemed to surprise both of them.

Nina studied him. Then she looked over her shoulder. "Clara?"

A moment passed.

Then Clara appeared at the top of the stairs.

Adrian forgot how to breathe.

She wore loose grey trousers and a soft white jumper. Her hair was tied back. Her face looked pale, tired, and more beautiful to him than anything he owned. He had seen her dressed for galas, jewels at her throat, lips painted, laughing under chandeliers. This hurt more. This was Clara without performance, and he had not been allowed to see her for six days.

"Adrian," she said.

He hated the distance in her voice.

"Come home."

Nina made a sound of disgust.

Clara came down two steps. "No."

The refusal struck him because some savage, arrogant part of him had expected his presence to matter enough. He had imagined anger. Tears. A fight. Her falling into his arms perhaps, because his panic had dressed itself as romance on the drive over.

No was clean. No had a door behind it.

"Clara, I am not signing those papers."

"You don't have to sign immediately. My solicitor explained the process."

"Don't talk to me about process."

"Why? You love process."

He flinched.

She came down another step. Nina stayed where she was, a guard dog in silk.
