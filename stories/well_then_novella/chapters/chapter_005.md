# Chapter 5: Address on the Papers

The divorce papers arrived six days later.

Adrian was in the Chelsea library, unshaven, wearing yesterday's shirt, reading an email he had not absorbed, when the courier came. Legal envelope. Heavy cream paper. His name typed with professional indifference.

He knew before he opened it.

Still, the words hit like violence.

Petition for dissolution of marriage.

Clara Elizabeth Vale, petitioner.

Adrian Nicholas Vale, respondent.

He read nothing else at first. His eyes fixed on her name, then on the address listed for service.

Nina Hart's house.

His body moved before thought finished forming.

He took his coat. His keys. Clara's ring from his pocket, because he had carried it there every day since she left. His driver was off duty; Adrian drove himself, badly and too fast, through wet London streets until he reached Islington close to midnight.

Nina Hart opened the door wearing a dressing gown and the expression of a woman who had expected him eventually.

"No," she said.

Adrian stood on the step, rain in his hair. "I need to see my wife."

"Your wife needs sleep."

"Nina."

"Mr. Vale."

The correction was deliberate. It landed.

He looked past her into the narrow hall. Warm light. Shoes by the stairs. A small cat glaring from a radiator. Ordinary life, closing ranks around Clara.

"Tell her I'm here."

"She knows."

His breath changed.

"Then let me speak to her."

Nina folded her arms. "You are not coming into my house to drag a woman into a midnight argument because the papers scared you."

"I have never dragged Clara anywhere."

"No. You just left her standing alone until she moved herself."

He absorbed that because he deserved it.

"Please," he said.

That word seemed to surprise both of them.

Nina studied him. Then she looked over her shoulder. "Clara?"

A moment passed.

Then Clara appeared at the top of the stairs.

Adrian forgot how to breathe.

She wore loose grey trousers and a soft white jumper. Her hair was tied back. Her face looked pale, tired, and more beautiful to him than anything he owned. He had seen her dressed for galas, jewels at her throat, lips painted, laughing under chandeliers. This hurt more. This was Clara without performance, and he had not been allowed to see her for six days.

"Adrian," she said.

He hated the distance in her voice.

"Come home."

Nina made a sound of disgust.

Clara came down two steps. "No."

The refusal struck him because some savage, arrogant part of him had expected his presence to matter enough. He had imagined anger. Tears. A fight. Her falling into his arms perhaps, because his panic had dressed itself as romance on the drive over.

No was clean. No had a door behind it.

"Clara, I am not signing those papers."

"You don't have to sign immediately. My solicitor explained the process."

"Don't talk to me about process."

"Why? You love process."

He flinched.

She came down another step. Nina stayed where she was, a guard dog in silk.

"I did not sleep with Celine," Adrian said.

"I know."

That stopped him.

"You know?"

"I know you well enough. If you had, you would have looked guiltier and less offended."

His mouth opened. Closed.

Clara's voice remained steady. "I am not divorcing you because I think you had sex with her."

"Then why?"

Her eyes filled, and that was worse than anger.

"Because when I saw her touching you, it made sense."

The sentence entered him slowly.

Clara gripped the banister. "It made sense, Adrian. That is what you should be worried about. Not whether you crossed the final line. Not whether a lawyer could prove adultery. I saw another woman with her hand on you, and my first thought was not impossible. My first thought was of course."

He could not speak.

"You made me live in a marriage where that looked believable."

Nina's face softened. Adrian barely saw it.

He looked at his wife and understood that denial had been protecting the wrong part of him. He wanted to argue the facts because the facts were survivable. He had not kissed Celine. He had not taken her to bed. He had not broken that vow.

But he had broken something less legal and more intimate every day for months.

"I forgot your birthday," he said.

Clara laughed once, without humour. "You forgot more than that."

"I know."

"No, you don't. You know now that I left. That isn't the same thing."

Rain ran down the back of his neck. He did not move.

"Tell me," he said.

"No."

"Clara."

"No," she repeated. "I have spent years making it easy for you to love me lazily. I reminded you of dinners. I accepted apologies before you finished making them. I smiled at your staff parties, made your family feel remembered, packed your shirts, learned your moods, forgave every late night because you were building something important. And somewhere along the way you decided I was part of the furniture. Useful. Expensive. Always there."

"I never thought that."

"You behaved it."

That silenced him again.

She descended to the hall but stayed behind Nina, as if even closeness required a witness.

His hand went to his coat pocket. He took out her ring.

Clara's face broke for half a second before she steadied it.

"Don't," she said.

He looked down at the ring in his palm.

He had imagined giving it back as a declaration. As proof. He saw now how cheap that would be, one more object offered where attention had failed.

He closed his fingers around it.

"I don't know how to fix this," he said.

"Good."

His gaze lifted.

Her eyes were wet and merciless. "Maybe if you don't know, you'll stop assuming one grand gesture can do it."

Behind her, Nina looked frankly impressed.

Adrian nodded once. The movement felt like lowering a weapon.

"Will you come home tonight?"

"No."

"Tomorrow?"

"No."

"Can I see you?"

She hesitated.

Hope rose in him so fast it hurt.

"You can write to my solicitor."

"Clara."

Her mouth trembled. "Do you know how many nights I waited for you?"

He did not answer.

"Now you can wait."

Nina opened the door wider, not inviting him in, but making space for him to leave.

Adrian looked at Clara for one more second. He wanted to cross the hall, take her face in his hands, make her remember his mouth, his body, the heat that had always called heat from her. The wanting was immediate and brutal.

But he saw her fingers tight on the banister.

He saw that taking one step would prove her point.

So he stepped back into the rain.

"I love you," he said.

Clara closed her eyes.

"I know," she said. "That's never been the whole problem."

Nina shut the door.

Adrian stood outside until the hall light went dark.
