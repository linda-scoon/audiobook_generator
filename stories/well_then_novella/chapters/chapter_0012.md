# Chapter 12: Anniversary, Rewritten

Six months later, Adrian was late to dinner.

Clara sat at the kitchen table in the Chelsea house with two glasses of wine, a bowl of pasta that was not sticking together, and one eyebrow raised at the empty chair opposite her.

At 7:04, her phone buzzed.

I am downstairs. There is a situation.

She stared at the message.

Then another came.

The situation is flowers. Too many. I panicked at the florist.

Clara laughed so loudly the housekeeper smiled in the hallway.

The front door opened. Adrian entered carrying an absurd number of roses, tulips, and something purple that looked expensive and confused. He had rain on his coat and apology on his face, but not the old careless kind. This one arrived before defence.

"Four minutes," he said. "I know."

"The empire survives?"

"The empire has a COO and strict instructions not to contact me unless there is fire, blood, prison, or you calling them personally to report my death."

"Comprehensive."

He set the flowers down. "I also bought cake."

"Of course you did."

"Professionally made this time. I respect your stomach too much to repeat November."

She rose and crossed the hall to him.

He watched her come with the same attention that had once startled her at the charity auction. It had not faded when she returned. That had been the true test.

Change, Clara had learned, was not proven in crisis. Crisis made men dramatic. Change was proven on ordinary Tuesdays, in answered messages, in calendars protected, in walking away from flirtation before it became flattering, in noticing when her voice went quiet and asking the question before she had to bleed for it.

Adrian still worked too much sometimes. Clara still bristled too quickly when a meeting ran over. They had argued in January about Singapore, in February about his mother, in March about the foundation budget, and last week about whether buying a cottage in Wales because she once said misty hills were romantic counted as listening or overcompensating.

Marriage had not become effortless.

It had become mutual.

She stopped in front of him and straightened his tie.

His eyes changed.

They both remembered another woman's hand there.

Clara held his gaze while she finished the knot.

"There," she said. "Much better. You looked almost human for a moment."

Adrian's face softened with pain and humour at once.

"I deserved that."

"You did."

"Are we all right?"

She touched the tie once more, then flattened her hand over his heart.

"Yes."

He covered her hand with his. "Good."

Dinner was warm. The candles were new, tall and steady. He put his phone in a drawer before sitting down, and when it buzzed once against the wood, they both ignored it.

After pasta, after wine, after cake eaten straight from the box because plates felt unnecessary, Adrian gave her a small envelope.

Clara eyed it. "This better not be property."

"It is paper."

"That is not reassuring from a man who can buy islands."

"No island."

She opened it.

Inside was a reservation confirmation for the bakery near their first flat. Private lesson for two. Pistachio cake, advanced level.

She looked up slowly. "Advanced?"

"I intend to become adequate."

"Ambitious."

"I built a multinational company."

"And failed pasta."

"A humbling data point."

She smiled, then saw the second paper behind the reservation.

It was the withdrawal confirmation from her solicitor. The divorce petition formally discontinued.

Her chest tightened.

"I thought you might want to keep a copy," Adrian said quietly. "Not as pressure. As proof that you chose the ending."

Clara ran her fingers over the paper.

She had signed the withdrawal two weeks earlier. She had expected relief, and felt it. But seeing it here, at their table, with him watching her as though choice still humbled him, made it real in a different way.

"I did," she said.

"Yes."

"You know I can still leave if you become an idiot again."

"I live in motivating fear."

"Good."

He laughed.

Later, upstairs, Clara changed into the blue dress from the birthday he had forgotten. She had not planned to. It hung in the wardrobe like a question until she answered it. When she came out, Adrian was standing by the window, jacket off, sleeves rolled, city lights behind him.

He turned.

Everything in him stopped.

The reaction moved through her like a hand down her spine.

"Clara," he said.

"You remember this dress?"

His gaze lifted from the dress to her face.

"Yes."

"Tell me."

He came toward her slowly. "You wore it on your birthday. I came home late. You had waited. I made the first apology too small because I still thought the injury was one evening."

She swallowed.

"And now?"

"Now I know it was one evening where the whole injury became visible."

Her eyes burned.

"Good answer."

"I have been studying."

"Come here, then."

He did.

She expected him to touch the dress. Instead, he touched her face.

"Happy birthday," he said.

"It's not my birthday."

"I know. I am still late."

The tears came fast enough to annoy her.

Adrian brushed them away with his thumbs.

"I love you," he said.

"I love you too."

His breath caught like it still surprised him.

That was one thing she liked. He never treated the answer as automatic anymore.

She kissed him first.

The kiss began softly, then changed. Six months had not made his desire polite. It had only made it safer. He still wanted with his whole body, still touched her like possession was an instinct he had taught manners but not killed. Clara liked that. She liked the controlled hunger in him, liked the way his hands tightened when she gave permission, liked knowing he would stop if she asked and continue if she smiled against his mouth and pulled him closer.

"Take off the dress," she said.

His eyes darkened. "Are you sure?"

"Adrian."

"Yes?"

"If you ask that in that careful voice every time, I am going to start weaponizing it."

He smiled. "You already do."

Then he turned her gently and lowered the zip.

The dress slid down her arms, over her hips, to the floor. Adrian followed it to his knees, pressing his mouth to her spine, her waist, the curve of her hip. Clara looked down at him, at the dark head bent to her body, and remembered another night when she had placed her ring on his bedside table because she could not make him see her.

Now he looked up as if looking was the point of him.

"Beautiful," he said.

She believed he meant more than her skin.

That was why she let him take his time.

He undressed her with a patience that became its own form of heat. Mouth on breast, hand on thigh, lips at the inside of her knee. By the time he lifted her onto the bed, she was laughing under her breath from wanting and frustration.

"You are enjoying yourself," she accused.

"Immensely."

"I can tell."

"Good. I would hate to be subtle with my wife."

My wife still warmed her. Not because law trapped her there. Because choice had returned the word to her.

She pulled him down.

The last of his restraint broke with a low sound. He came over her, around her, into the space she made for him, and the bed became theirs again without ghosts. He touched her where she asked, kissed her where she arched, entered her only when she hooked her leg around his hip and said now in the voice that ruined him.

Then there was no more cleverness.

There was heat, hard and sweet. His body over hers. Her hands on his back. The rhythm they had rebuilt not from habit but from attention. He watched her face until pleasure made her eyes close, then kissed them open again because he knew her now and wanted all of her present.

Clara came with his name in her mouth and his hand locked with hers above her head.

Adrian followed, shaken and silent afterward, his face against her throat.

For a while they stayed like that.

Then Clara said, "The flowers downstairs are ridiculous."

His laugh warmed her skin. "I know."

"No more panic flowers."

"Noted."

"And no Welsh cottage."

A pause.

"Define no."

She pushed at his shoulder.

He rolled, taking her with him, and she landed sprawled across his chest. Her ring caught the low light.

Adrian touched it once, then left his hand open beside hers.

Not covering. Not holding down. Just there.

Clara slid her fingers through his.

The house was quiet around them. Not empty. Not waiting.

Full.

Downstairs, candles burned on the kitchen table. His phone sat unanswered in a drawer. Rain tapped the windows, and the flowers leaned absurdly over the hall table, too many of them, all alive.

Clara closed her eyes.

"Well then," she said softly.

Adrian's chest moved beneath her.

"Well then," he answered.
