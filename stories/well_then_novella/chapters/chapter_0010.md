# Chapter 10: The House by the Sea

Dinner was almost inedible.

Adrian burned the garlic, undercooked the pasta, and discovered too late that reading a recipe once did not equal understanding it. Clara sat at the kitchen island wearing one of his shirts and watched him wage war against a pan.

"You own restaurants," she said.

"I invest in restaurants. Apparently that skill is not transferable."

"The pasta is sticking."

"I am aware."

"Are you?"

He pointed the wooden spoon at her. "You agreed to insult it after."

"I am offering live support."

"This is heckling."

She smiled into her wine.

For a moment, they were almost themselves.

Then she went back to Nina's.

Adrian kissed her once at the door, because she allowed it, and watched the car pull away with a steadier face than the first time. Clara cried in the back seat anyway.

The next morning, she expected regret.

Regret came, but it was not simple. She did not regret sleeping with him. She regretted how easy it would be to let desire do the hard work for them. Adrian had touched her beautifully. He had listened. He had cooked badly and let her leave. Those things mattered.

They were still not a marriage.

When he asked for dinner again, she said no.

When he asked for coffee, she said yes.

When he asked whether she would consider a weekend at the house by the sea, separate rooms, no staff except Noel preparing it and leaving, she did not answer for two days.

Nina said, "Absolutely not."

Clara said, "I didn't ask you."

"You told me, which is worse."

"We need to talk somewhere that isn't a restaurant or this house."

"Talk in a park. Lots of witnesses. Ducks. Very romantic."

Clara leaned back against the kitchen counter. "I miss my life."

Nina softened. "I know."

"Not the money. Not the houses. I miss him. I miss us when we were good. I need to know if that's still real or if we're just good at wanting each other."

So she said yes to the sea house.

Noel prepared it with the discretion of a man who knew more about their marriage than he wanted to. The house stood on a private stretch of Cornish coast, all grey stone, salt-streaked glass, and wind-bent grass. Adrian had bought it after Clara mentioned once that the sea made her feel honest.

He remembered that. Of course he did. He had always remembered dramatically. It was daily remembering he had failed.

They arrived in separate cars.

Noel met Clara at the door.

"Mrs. Vale."

"Noel."

For a second they both remembered the rain, the taxi, well then.

His mouth almost moved.

She saved him. "Thank you for preparing the house."

"Of course. Pantry is stocked. Heating is behaving for once. Mr. Vale asked me to tell you both that I will be at the village inn if needed, but otherwise invisible."

"He asked you to say invisible?"

"No. That was my contribution."

She smiled.

Adrian arrived twenty minutes later, looking windblown and uncertain in a black jumper instead of a suit. Clara had forgotten how young he could look out of armour.

They walked through the house together. Separate rooms, as promised. Hers facing the water, his down the hall.

"I can take the room downstairs," he said.

"I know where you are. A staircase won't save me if I lose my mind."

His eyes warmed. "Are we joking about that?"

"A little."

"Good. I missed your cruelty."

She turned away before he saw her smile too clearly.

That first afternoon, they talked.

Not elegantly. Not like people in films who knew exactly how to confess. They sat at the kitchen table while rain hit the windows and went through the marriage like sorting broken glass.

Clara spoke about loneliness, humiliation, the slow embarrassment of asking for the same thing until asking became another wound. Adrian listened. Sometimes he tried to answer too quickly; she stopped him. Sometimes he looked so stricken she wanted to soften the truth; she forced herself not to.

"I started making myself smaller," she said. "Not because you asked me to. Because wanting more from you made me feel needy."

Adrian's face tightened.

"I did that," he said.

"Yes."

"I don't want you small."

"Wanting isn't enough."

"No."

He spoke too. Less fluently than in boardrooms. He admitted that success had become a machine he fed because stopping scared him. That being admired publicly was easier than being needed privately. That he had confused Clara's competence with contentment.

"You made marriage look effortless," he said.

"Because I was doing the effort."

"Yes."

The admission sat between them.

By evening, they were exhausted.

Adrian cooked again. Better this time. Fish, potatoes, salad. No garlic casualties.

"Noel arranged half of this," Clara said.

"All of it," Adrian admitted. "But I put heat under things."

"Growth."

After dinner, they watched an old film under separate blankets on the same sofa. Halfway through, Clara's foot touched his thigh by accident.

Neither of them moved.

Then Adrian placed his hand lightly over her ankle.

She let it stay.

The second day, they walked along the beach in heavy coats. Wind tangled Clara's hair. Adrian looked at her more than the sea.

"Stop," she said.

"Looking?"

"Like you're composing a tragic poem."

"I would write a terrible poem."

"I know. That's why I'm stopping you."

He laughed. The sound moved through her chest like memory.

On the way back, rain caught them hard and sudden. They ran for the house, breathless and soaked, and Clara slipped on the wet stone step. Adrian caught her around the waist.

The contact changed everything.

For one suspended second, rain slid down his face and her hands were flat against his chest. His body was warm through wet wool. His eyes dropped to her mouth.

"Clara," he said.

The warning again.

She was tired of warnings.

She kissed him.

Inside, they left wet clothes in a trail from the back door to the downstairs bathroom, laughing at first, then not laughing at all when Adrian pressed her against the locked door and sank to his knees.

He looked up at her from there, hands on her thighs, rain still in his hair.

"Tell me no," he said, voice rough, "and I stop."

Her fingers tightened in his hair.

"If you stop, I may never forgive you."

His smile was brief and wicked.

Then his mouth was on her.

Clara's head hit the door. Pleasure tore through her too quickly, sharpened by weeks of restraint and the sight of him kneeling fully dressed between her legs as though no boardroom, no fortune, no empire had ever taught him pride. He took his time anyway. He made her say what she wanted. Made her repeat it when she tried to hide in gasps. He praised her shamelessly, low words against wet skin, until she came with one hand over her mouth and the other locked in his hair.

He rose slowly, kissing her stomach, her ribs, the curve of her breast.

"Upstairs," she said.

"Yes, Mrs. Vale."

The title should not have affected her.

It did.

They barely made it to her room. This time there was no anger in the first touch, only hunger made honest by everything said at the kitchen table. Adrian stripped her carefully, then himself less carefully because her hands were impatient. He laid her on the bed facing the sea and followed her down.

The sex was slower than at Chelsea. Worse for that. Better. He took her with his mouth on hers, with her hands pinned above her head only after she put them there herself and dared him with her eyes. He asked once, she answered once, and then there was no room for language that was not breath, skin, yes, again, Adrian.

When he moved inside her, Clara looked at him and did not hide.

His control nearly broke.

"I love you," he said, voice shaking.

She touched his face.

"Then keep learning me."

He lowered his forehead to hers and did.

Later, tangled in damp sheets while rain blurred the windows, Clara did not say she would come home.

Adrian did not ask.

That was why she stayed the night.
