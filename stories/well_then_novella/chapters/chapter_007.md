# Chapter 7: Public Things

Adrian's first real request came through the solicitor three weeks after Clara left.

Not home. Not dinner alone. Not please.

A charity auction at the Vales' foundation, scheduled months earlier, with both their names on the invitation.

Mrs. Vale,

Mr. Vale asks whether you would be willing to attend the foundation auction in your independent capacity as co-chair. He will arrange separate transport, separate arrival, and no private conversation unless you initiate it. He says the staff have prepared for your role and should not be penalised for his failures.

Clara hated how fair that was.

Nina hated it too. "Manipulative."

"Maybe."

"Effective manipulation is still manipulation."

Clara read the email again. "The foundation matters to me."

"So go for the foundation. Not for him."

That was what Clara told herself when she put on a black satin dress Adrian had once unzipped with his teeth after a gala in Vienna.

She should not have chosen that dress.

She chose it anyway.

The auction was held in a converted bank near the Strand, all pale stone, glass, and money pretending to be virtue. Cameras flashed at the entrance. Clara arrived alone and felt the ripple go through the waiting press. Mrs. Vale without Mr. Vale. No wedding ring. Chin lifted.

Adrian stood inside near the main staircase.

He saw her and stopped speaking mid-sentence.

The effect on him was visible enough that the woman beside him turned to look.

Clara felt it across the room: his attention, full and undiluted, landing on her like heat.

He did not approach.

That mattered.

She greeted trustees, donors, staff. She made small talk. She smiled until her cheeks ached. Adrian remained where duty placed him, but she felt him tracking her. Not possessively in the old careless way. Carefully. As if every step toward her had to be earned.

During the first speeches, she stood three people away from him. Close enough to smell his cologne. Close enough to see the tired shadows under his eyes.

He had lost weight.

Good, a petty part of her thought.

Then, softer, Oh.

His speech was brief. He thanked the staff first, then the donors, then Clara.

Not my wife.

Clara Vale, without whom this foundation would have remained an impressive tax structure instead of useful work.

There was a small laugh from the room. Clara did not laugh. She looked at him.

He looked back.

No smile. No performance. Just acknowledgement, offered publicly where he had once failed privately.

Later, near the silent auction tables, an older donor cornered Clara beside a display of photographs.

"Such a shame," Lady Ashcombe said, touching Clara's arm with false sympathy. "These modern marriages. So much drama. In my day, wives understood that important men had pressures."

Clara's spine stiffened.

Before she could answer, Adrian's voice came from behind her.

"In your day, Lady Ashcombe, important men were often indulged into becoming selfish ones. I am trying to recover from the tradition."

Lady Ashcombe turned scarlet.

Clara stared at the photograph in front of her because looking at him would be dangerous.

"Adrian," the older woman said, brittle. "I only meant that marriage requires patience."

"Yes," he said. "My wife had plenty. I exhausted it."

My wife.

The words moved through Clara in a place she had not given permission.

Lady Ashcombe retreated.

Adrian remained a careful distance away.

"I didn't mean to interrupt," he said. "Actually, I did. But not to crowd you."

Clara turned.

Up close, he looked worse. Controlled, immaculate, and suffering under the surface.

"Thank you," she said.

"She was wrong."

"Yes."

"I used to enjoy her approval. That is embarrassing in retrospect."

Clara almost smiled.

His eyes moved over her face, caught the almost, and warmed with such naked hunger for any sign from her that she had to look away.

A waiter passed with champagne. Clara took a glass. Adrian did not.

"You look beautiful," he said.

The words were quiet, simple, and therefore more dangerous than if he had made them grand.

"Don't."

"All right."

He stopped at once.

That startled her.

He noticed. "I am learning to hear the first no."

The sentence held no self-pity. No request for praise.

Clara took a sip of champagne because her mouth had gone dry.

"The Celine story travelled," she said.

"Good."

"You embarrassed her."

"She earned some. I earned more."

Clara looked at him then.

He held her gaze. "I should have made the boundary visible before you had to wonder whether it existed."

The room seemed too warm.

She remembered the dream. His hands. The dresser. His mouth.

He must have seen something shift in her face because his attention sharpened. Not triumphant. Not smug. Male in a way that made her pulse change despite every sensible thought she owned.

"Clara," he said, very softly.

Her name became touch.

She hated him for still being able to do that.

"I need air," she said.

She set down the glass and walked toward the terrace doors.

He did not follow immediately. She counted the seconds because apparently she had become pathetic.

One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Six. Seven.

The door opened behind her.

"I waited," he said.

She laughed despite herself. It came out breathless. "Seven seconds?"

"It was heroic."

She should not have laughed again. She did.

The terrace overlooked the river. London shone wet and electric beyond the stone balustrade. Cold air moved over her bare shoulders. Adrian removed his jacket, then stopped.

"May I?"

That almost undid her.

She nodded.

He placed the jacket around her shoulders without letting his hands linger. The lining held his warmth. His scent rose around her, clean, dark, familiar.

Clara gripped the edges of the jacket.

"I miss you," he said.

She closed her eyes.

"Don't make me comfort you."

"I won't."

"I mean it."

"I know. I miss you. That is my problem to carry. I only wanted it said without pretending dignity is the same as honesty."

She opened her eyes.

He stood beside her, not touching the railing, hands in his pockets as if he had put them there for discipline.

"I miss you too," she said, and felt the cost of it. "That is not forgiveness."

His breath left him slowly.

"No."

"It is not an invitation."

"I understand."

She looked at him. "Do you?"

His mouth curved without humour. "I understand that if I were the man I was four weeks ago, I would kiss you now and trust your body to argue for me."

Her pulse jumped.

"And now?"

"Now I know your body was never the part of you I neglected."

There it was again. The dangerous fairness of him when he stopped trying to win and started telling the truth.

Clara turned back to the river.

"I don't know what to do with you like this."

"Good," Adrian said. "You told me not knowing might help."

She smiled before she could stop herself.

He saw it. She knew because his whole face changed, not much, only enough to show the man she had married beneath the one who had lost her.

For one foolish second, she wanted his hands on her so badly the cold disappeared.

Then the terrace door opened, and a trustee looked out.

"Mrs. Vale? They're asking for you inside."

Clara handed Adrian his jacket without looking at him.

Their fingers brushed.

A small contact. Nothing.

Heat went through both of them.

Adrian's hand closed around the jacket very slowly.

"Clara," he said.

She looked at his mouth. That was the mistake.

"Goodnight, Adrian."

She went inside.

This time, he let her go.

But that night, alone in Nina's spare bed, Clara touched herself with her eyes closed and Adrian's jacket still on the chair across the room. She came silently, angrily, with his name bitten between her teeth.

Across London, Adrian sat on the edge of his bed with her ring in his hand and did not trust himself to imagine what he wanted.

Not yet.
