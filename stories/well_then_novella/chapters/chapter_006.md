# Chapter 6: What She Would Not Give Him

Clara expected relief after sending him away.

Instead, she shook so badly Nina made her sit on the bottom stair and put her head between her knees.

"Breathe," Nina said.

"I hate this."

"I know."

"I wanted to go with him."

"I know that too."

Clara looked up, humiliated.

Nina sat beside her. "Wanting him doesn't mean he gets you."

That sentence became the first solid thing Clara held.

The next morning, Adrian sent no flowers.

She hated that she noticed. Then hated that she respected it.

At ten, her solicitor forwarded a message.

Mrs. Vale,

Mr. Vale has requested permission to send one letter directly to you. I have advised him that all legal communication must continue through this office. He says the letter is not legal in nature. Please advise whether you consent to receive it.

Clara stared at the email for fifteen minutes.

Nina, reading over her shoulder without shame, said, "Letters are slower than ambushes."

So Clara consented.

The letter arrived by courier that afternoon. Not typed. Adrian's handwriting, controlled and black, covered three pages.

Clara read it alone.

Clara,

I began three versions of this letter by defending myself. I deleted them. You already know I did not sleep with Celine. Repeating that fact is cowardice if I use it to avoid the larger truth.

I liked being admired. That is an ugly thing to write. I liked that she looked at me as if I was impressive and uncomplicated. I allowed closeness I would have hated any man for allowing with you. I told myself intention mattered more than conduct because that made me innocent in my own mind. It did not make me faithful in the ways you deserved.

You said seeing her touch me made sense. I have not been able to stop hearing that.

Then he listed things.

Not dramatic things. Worse. Specific ones.

Your birthday. Our anniversary. The dinner in March when you waited in the blue dress and I took the Dubai call. Your exhibition opening, where I arrived after your speech and told myself arriving at all counted. The weekend at the lake when I spent six hours on calls and you swam alone. The morning you asked whether I still liked the Chelsea house and I answered an email instead of you.

Clara cried then, quietly, because he remembered. Or had gone looking. Either way, he had finally turned his attention toward the wreckage.

The letter did not ask her to come home.

At the end, he wrote:

I want to ask for another chance. I know I have not earned the right to ask. So I will not put the burden of answering on you today. I will use my time better than I used yours.

A.

Nina read it after Clara allowed her.

"Not bad," she said grudgingly.

"No."

"Still not enough."

"No."

Clara folded the letter and put it in the drawer by the spare bed.

Three days later, Adrian sent another. Shorter.

Today I learned the housekeeper's daughter's name is Priya, not Prita. You corrected me twice last year. I smiled and forgot. I am writing it here because I don't want the correction to disappear again.

Clara did not cry over that one. She laughed, then cried because she had laughed.

The letters came every other day. Never begging. Never romantic in the easy way. They were records of attention, each one a small admission of blindness.

I cancelled the Zurich trip. Not moved. Cancelled.

I took the board call from home and ended it at six. There was no emergency afterward. I have been misusing that word.

I found the black coat you repaired for me. I had thanked Gemma for arranging it. I am sorry.

Celine March has been removed from direct negotiations. The partnership may fail. That is acceptable.

Clara read each letter. She answered none.

Meanwhile, Adrian suffered in ways money could not outsource.

The house remained functional, but function mocked him. Staff could feed him, clean for him, schedule him, drive him. They could not look up from a book and smile because he had entered the room. They could not tell him his tie was too severe for dinner with her mother. They could not press cold feet against his leg in bed and then deny doing it.

He began noticing the shape of evenings.

Previously, evenings had been what happened after work if work allowed. Now they were rooms he had to survive.

He tried sleeping in the bedroom again and lasted one night. At 2:40 a.m., he woke from a dream in which Clara was laughing in the shower, and the silence beside him was so complete he got up and went downstairs like a man evicted by his own bed.

On the twelfth day of her absence, he went to his office for a board meeting and found Celine waiting in reception.

Gemma intercepted him before he reached her. "She doesn't have an appointment. I told security. She insisted on waiting."

Adrian looked across the lobby.

Celine smiled as if nothing serious had happened. "Adrian."

He did not go to her. He spoke to security.

"Ms. March is not cleared for this floor. Please escort her out."

Her smile faltered. "Are you serious?"

"Yes."

People looked. Adrian let them.

Celine's face hardened. "You're making a mistake."

"I made it already. I'm correcting it."

"Over a wife who ran away?"

He crossed the lobby then, not close enough for intimacy, close enough for finality.

"My wife left a marriage I had neglected. You saw a weakness and pressed on it. That is partly my failure. It will not become my habit."

Her cheeks flushed.

"How touching," she said.

"No. Touching was the problem."

A few people pretended not to hear. Gemma looked down at her tablet with suspicious intensity.

Celine left.

By lunch, the story had travelled through the company in polished, abbreviated form. Adrian did not correct it. Let them know. Let every person who had seen Celine lean over his desk now see her removed.

That evening, he wrote to Clara and told her exactly what had happened.

He almost did not send it because it sounded like asking for credit.

Then he added:

I am not telling you this so you will reward me. I am telling you because hiding small things helped create this. I should have done it before you were hurt.

Clara read the letter three times.

Her body remembered Adrian at the door in the rain. The wet hair. The ring in his hand. The restraint when he wanted to move toward her and did not.

Wanting him did not mean he got her.

But not wanting him had never been the truth.

That night she dreamed of him.

Not the office. Not Celine. Not even the forgotten birthday.

She dreamed of his hands on her waist in the mirror, his mouth near her shoulder, the low command of come to bed. In the dream she did not say she was tired. She turned. He lifted her onto the dresser, pushed her skirt up with impatient hands, and kissed her like a man who knew exactly what he had almost lost.

Clara woke hot, angry, and alone.

"Absolutely not," she whispered to the ceiling.

The cat, sleeping at the foot of the bed, opened one eye and judged her.

Clara threw back the covers and went to make tea.

At breakfast, Nina took one look at her and said, "You had a sex dream about your husband."

Clara choked.

"I did not."

"Please. You look furious and under-satisfied."

"I hate you."

"You love me. Also, don't call him."

"I wasn't going to call him."

Nina buttered her toast. "I didn't say don't want to. I said don't call."

Clara said nothing.

That was answer enough.
