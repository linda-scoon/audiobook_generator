# Chapter 8: The Work of Grovelling

Grovelling, Adrian discovered, was not a speech.

It was administration.

It was calendar surgery. It was telling shareholders no. It was learning which florist Clara disliked because their lilies died too quickly and which dry cleaner had ruined her silver dress. It was asking Gemma how often Clara had called the office to move his life around and watching his assistant hesitate before answering honestly.

"A lot," Gemma said.

Adrian sat behind his desk. "Define a lot."

"Enough that when she stopped, the week became noticeably harder."

He nodded. "Thank you."

"She never made it feel like work," Gemma added, softer.

"No," Adrian said. "That was part of the problem."

He hired a chief operating officer he should have hired two years earlier. He moved himself out of daily approvals. He took weekends off and discovered the company did not collapse. He attended therapy, which he initially considered inefficient until the therapist asked why being needed by everyone except his wife had seemed preferable to being known by her.

He did not enjoy that hour.

He went back the next week.

Clara heard pieces through solicitors, gossip, and Nina's shameless surveillance of business pages.

"He hired a COO," Nina announced one morning. "Shares dipped point three percent, then recovered. Very tragic."

Clara stirred honey into tea. "Good."

"Also, Celine March's firm has withdrawn from the deal."

Clara stopped stirring.

"Is Vale Meridian hurt?"

"Probably. Do we care?"

Clara gave her a look.

Nina sighed. "Fine. Some analysts say short-term disruption, long-term negligible. He'll survive. The poor billionaire will still have many billions."

Clara looked down at the tea.

She was relieved. That irritated her. She had wanted consequences but not ruin. Anger did not make love vanish; it only made love harder to place.

On Friday, Adrian sent a package through her solicitor.

Inside was not jewellery. Not a key. Not a sentimental object.

It was a proposed separation agreement.

Nina read it first because Clara's hands had gone cold.

"Well," Nina said after a while. "That is either romantic or insane."

"What?"

"He gives you the Chelsea house outright if you want it. The lake house too. Independent settlement, no confidentiality clause, no challenge to the divorce if you continue after six months. He'll pay your legal fees. He waives any claim to jewellery, gifts, foundation position, and... oh."

"What?"

Nina's voice changed. "He agrees not to contact you directly unless you initiate, except one written letter per week, and no visits without permission."

Clara sat down.

There was a note clipped to the front.

Clara,

I do not want a divorce. I am not pretending otherwise. But I have controlled too much by assuming the world should arrange itself around my wanting. These terms are my attempt to make leaving me materially easy, so that if you stay, it is not because I trapped you with comfort, property, or pressure.

I hope you don't sign anything except a dinner reservation with me someday. But the choice should cost me more than it costs you.

A.

Clara pressed the note to her mouth.

Nina was quiet for once.

"Damn him," she said finally. "That one is good."

Clara laughed and cried at the same time.

She did not sign. She did not answer. But that night, she took Adrian's letters from the drawer and read them all again in order.

The next week, she agreed to coffee.

Public place. Noon. One hour.

Adrian arrived ten minutes early and did not stand when she entered, because standing would make every person in the restaurant look. He only looked at her as though the room had gone silent around her.

"Clara."

"Adrian."

The waitress came. Coffee gave them both something to do with their hands.

For the first fifteen minutes, they spoke like polite acquaintances. Foundation matters. Her mother. His COO. Then Clara set down her cup.

"Did you like her?"

He did not pretend not to know. "Celine?"

"Yes."

"Not in the way you mean."

"Don't answer carefully. Answer honestly."

He looked down at his coffee, then back at her. "I liked how she made me feel."

The pain was sharp, but clean. "Powerful?"

"Uncomplicated. Admired without being required."

Clara's throat tightened.

"Did you want her?"

He inhaled slowly. "For moments. Physically. Vaguely. Not enough to pursue, but enough that I should have been ashamed before you saw it."

She appreciated the truth and hated every word of it.

"Did you ever compare us?"

"No." That came immediate. "Never. She was attention. You are my life. I treated attention more carefully because I assumed my life could not leave."

Clara looked out the window.

People passed with umbrellas. Ordinary noon continued.

"That's the kind of answer that would have made me forgive you too quickly a month ago," she said.

"And now?"

"Now I know better."

He nodded, accepting the strike.

"What do you want from me today?" she asked.

His hands tightened around the cup once, then loosened.

"Another coffee next week."

She had expected more. A plea. A speech. A demand hiding inside vulnerability.

"That's all?"

"No," he said. "But it is what I am asking for."

She studied him.

This version of Adrian frightened her more than the arrogant one because she could survive arrogance by resisting it. This Adrian was harder. This Adrian was learning the exact shape of the door instead of trying to break it down.

"Fine," she said.

His relief was quiet and devastating.

They had coffee the next week. Then dinner, with separate cars. Then a walk through a park where he kept his hands in his coat pockets until she took his arm because she was cold and tired of pretending she did not know its shape.

His body went still at the contact.

"Don't make it significant," she said.

"I would never."

"You're making it significant in your head."

"Violently."

She laughed. He smiled.

The smile did damage.

On the fifth week, she agreed to visit the Chelsea house to collect more clothes while he was there.

It was a mistake.

She knew as soon as she entered the bedroom.

The room smelled like him. The bed was made. Her ring sat in a small velvet-lined dish on his bedside table, not hidden, not displayed like a trap. Kept.

Adrian stood by the doorway as she opened drawers. He did not come in.

"You can wait downstairs," she said.

"I can."

He did not move.

She looked over her shoulder. "Adrian."

"I know. I'll go."

Still he stayed one more second too long.

The air changed.

Clara felt it in her skin. Weeks of restraint, anger, letters, public distance, nights alone with his name in her mouth, all of it gathered into the small space between them.

He saw it happen.

His voice dropped. "Tell me to leave."

She gripped the drawer handle.

"Leave."

He closed his eyes.

Then he stepped back.

He actually stepped back.

Clara hated him for it.

"I will be downstairs," he said.

The moment he turned, she said, "Wait."

He stopped.

Her heart beat so hard it hurt.

This was the line she had been defending for weeks, and no law or friend or principle could stand at it for her now. Only her choice.

Adrian did not turn around.

"Clara," he said, rough. "Be careful."

The warning was not for him.

It was for her.

She crossed the room and touched his back with one hand.

His breath changed.

"I am still angry," she said.

"I know."

"This doesn't fix us."

"I know."

"If you treat this like winning, I will walk out and never come back."

He turned then.

His eyes were dark, starved, and entirely on her.

"Then I won't win," he said. "I'll worship."

Her body answered before pride could stop it.

She pulled him down by his shirt, and he kissed her like a man falling to his knees.
